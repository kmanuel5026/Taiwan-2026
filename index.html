Festivals & Events During Your Dates

November is one of the best months to visit Taiwan — dry, warm in the south, and stacked with cultural events. Your dates overlap with two major events perfectly: the Golden Horse Film Festival (Taiwan's equivalent of the Oscars for Chinese-language cinema) runs the entire time you're in Taipei, and the Tainan Jazz Festival lands exactly as you arrive in Tainan.

🎬 Golden Horse Film Festival
Nov 6–22, 2026 · Taipei cinemas
Taiwan's most prestigious film event — the Oscars of Chinese-language cinema. Screenings across Taipei include the recurring Queer Utopia programme and international premieres. Book tickets at goldenhorse.org.tw about 2 weeks before. Evening screenings are easy to slot around sightseeing.
🎷 Tainan Jazz Festival
Nov 7–8, 2026 · Wu Garden, West Central District, Tainan
Outdoor multi-stage jazz festival in the heart of Tainan's historic district, walking distance from your hotel. Local and international acts, free or low-cost entry, social atmosphere. You arrive the evening of Nov 6 — the timing couldn't be better.
🌹 Rose Exhibition
Mid-November · Xinsheng Park, Taipei
Free outdoor flower exhibition at its peak during your return to Taipei. Beautiful and unhurried — easy to fold into Day 13 alongside Guandu Temple and a Golden Horse screening.
🎭 Pas'ta'ai (Saisiyat Dwarf Spirit Ritual)
~Nov 14–15, 2026 · Miaoli / Hsinchu counties
A rare biennial indigenous ceremony held only in even years — all-night traditional songs, dancing, and millet wine. Falls just after your departure. Worth knowing about; can't realistically be added to the route but is extraordinary context for your time in Taiwan.

Before You Go — Practical Essentials

Getting Around
EasyCard — pick up at the airport on arrival. Works on every MRT, all city buses, YouBike rentals, and convenience store payments. Top up at any MRT station or 7-Eleven.

Airport MRT — direct from TPE to Taipei Main Station, 35 min, NT$160. Runs every 15 min.

HSR (High Speed Rail) — book in advance via the THSR app (in English). Taipei→Tainan 1h40m (NT$1,350), Tainan→Kaohsiung 30 min by TRA regular train (NT$69).

Kaohsiung has its own MRT + LRT light rail. The Red Line is a single straight north-south line — very easy to navigate.
Language
English signage is excellent on Taipei MRT and HSR. Most urban restaurants have picture menus or QR codes. Kaohsiung MRT is fully bilingual.

Google Translate camera mode is your best tool for menus and signs throughout — point, translate, order.

Tainan is the spottiest for English — vendors are warm and patient with pointing. A few saved phrases in Mandarin help (xiè xiè = thank you, duō shǎo qián = how much?).

Taiwan is remarkably easy to navigate without Mandarin — especially with QR codes and apps.
Weather & What to Pack
Taipei (Nov 1–6, 12–14): 18–24°C. Light jacket for evenings; mid-month can cool quickly. Low rain probability.

Tainan / Kaohsiung (Nov 7–12): 22–28°C. Essentially no rain. T-shirts and light layers.

Hiking gear: Good walking shoes are essential — you'll cover 8–15km most days. Teapot Mountain requires ankle support.

Outdoor shopping: Do this on Day 2 (Taipei) before going south — you can check purchased items in luggage.

No umbrella needed for most of the trip, but a light packable one for Taipei doesn't hurt.
City One

Taipei

November 1–6 · 5 nights · arrival 9pm Nov 1

Taiwan's capital is a city of effortless layers — volcanic mountains rising above neon-lit night markets, one of Asia's most vibrant queer scenes anchored around the Red House in Ximending, and a constellation of hiking trails that put you above the skyline in under an hour. Taipei rewards wandering: every block has a temple alley, a craft café, or a mural you didn't expect. Staying in Ximending puts you at the centre of the queer scene, two minutes from the Red House, and one MRT stop from the airport train.

Stay: Ximending district — amba Taipei Ximending (mid-range, NT$3,500–6,000) · Comma Boutique Hotel (artsy, rooftop balcony) · CityInn Hotel Plus Ximending (budget, NT$1,500–2,500) · All within 10-min walk of Red House & Ximen MRT

Breakfast · Essential
Yong He Soy Milk King 永和豆漿大王 — No. 102, Section 2, Fuxing S. Rd, Da'an District. 10-min walk or 1 MRT stop from Ximending toward Longshan Temple.
Yong He Soy Milk King 永和豆漿大王 (Da'an)
📍 No. 102, Sec. 2, Fuxing S. Rd, Da'an District 🕐 5am–1:30pm daily 💰 NT$20–80 per item
Your first proper Taiwanese breakfast — and the right one. Yong He is the original template for traditional Taiwanese morning food: salty soy milk (鹹豆漿) — warm, savory, with dried shrimp, preserved vegetable, fried dough, and vinegar curdling it into a silky soup that's unlike anything you've had; youtiao (油條) — long, airy fried dough sticks served hot and crispy; dan bing (蛋餅) — thin egg crepe, savory and soft; radish cake (蘿蔔糕) — pan-fried with optional egg, deeply satisfying. English menu posted clearly. Order at the counter, pay, find a seat (share tables freely — everyone does). This is the whole Taiwanese breakfast experience in one place, for under NT$150 per person.
Go sweet soy milk OR salty soy milk — the salty version is the more interesting one if you haven't had it before. The youtiao dipped into either is mandatory.
8:00am
Temple
Walk to Longshan Temple (Longshan Temple MRT, Blue Line, 1 stop from the breakfast spot — or 10-min walk). Arrive by 8am for the chanting ceremony.
Longshan Temple 龍山寺
📍 Wanhua District 🕐 Open 6am–10pm daily 💰 Free 🚇 Longshan Temple MRT (Blue Line)
Built in 1738 by Fujian settlers, Longshan is the most important and atmospheric temple in Taipei — a syncretic space where Buddhist, Taoist, and folk deities coexist across multiple halls. Over 100 deities are enshrined, including Guanyin (goddess of mercy), Mazu (sea goddess), and the Rabbit God — the only deity in Taiwan specifically dedicated to same-sex relationships. The morning chanting ceremony at 8am is free, extraordinary, and genuinely moving: a full chorus of devotees in a haze of incense, the sound enveloping the entire courtyard. Go early for a peaceful experience before tourists arrive. The temple has been rebuilt after WWII bombing but the spiritual energy is entirely authentic.
Don't miss the Rabbit God (Tu Er Shen) shrine at the back left of the complex — the deity traditionally governing same-sex love. Particularly meaningful to visit.
Herb Alley (青草巷) runs directly adjacent — rows of traditional medicine vendors with dried roots, medicinal teas, and centuries of practice. The underground fortune teller arcade on Guangzhou St. is right below (language barrier but atmospheric).
9:30am
Heritage
Bopiliao Historical Block — 5-min walk east of the temple.
Bopiliao Historical Block 剝皮寮歷史街區
📍 5-min walk east of Longshan Temple 🕐 Tue–Sun 9am–6pm 💰 Free
Taipei's only completely intact Qing Dynasty and Japanese colonial streetscape — a row of red-brick arcades with carved windows and traditional wooden signboards, preserved exactly as they were in the 1800s. Unlike other heritage areas where modernity has crept in, Bopiliao was intentionally protected as a museum district. Small free exhibitions inside the restored buildings trace everyday Taipei life from the era of ox carts and merchant stalls. Quiet, photogenic, and takes about 45 minutes at a relaxed pace. Makes a striking contrast to the temple's living energy immediately beside it.
Best for photography in the morning light, when the brick textures glow warm. Bring a camera.
10:30am
Park · History
228 Peace Memorial Park — 15-min walk or 1 MRT stop east from Bopiliao (NTU Hospital or Zhongzheng Dist.).
228 Peace Memorial Park 二二八和平公園
📍 Near Taipei Main Station, Zhongzheng District 🕐 Open daily 6am–10pm 💰 Free (museum NT$30)
One of Taipei's most significant and beautiful parks — named for the February 28, 1947 massacre in which the Nationalist government killed thousands of Taiwanese civilians, an event that was suppressed and undiscussed for decades. The park is now a site of memory and reconciliation, with a memorial monument, the excellent 228 Memorial Museum (housed in a 1931 Japanese colonial radio station building), and beautifully maintained banyan trees, ponds, and pavilions. The Taiwan Governor-General's Office (now the Presidential Office Building) is right next door. This is one of those places that tells you something essential about the island's 20th-century history that nothing else does — and it's genuinely beautiful as a park, with local elderly residents doing taichi and feeding the koi.
The museum is small (30–45 min) but well-curated with English captions. Worth the NT$30. The outdoor park is lovely to walk through even if you skip the museum.

MRT or 10-min walk to outdoor gear district near Taipei Main Station. The Zhongxiao E. Rd / Zhongshan N. Rd roundabout has 5–7 specialist shops within a 5-minute walk of each other — the highest concentration of outdoor gear in Taiwan. Buy here and check it into your south-bound luggage on Day 6.
Outdoor Gear Cluster · Zhongxiao / Zhongshan Roundabout
📍 5-min walk east of Taipei Main Station Exit 7 🕐 Usually 10am–9pm

Deng Shan You (登山友) — been here since 1959, incredible discount rack on the 2nd floor (Hagglöfs, RAB, Deuter at sale prices). Patagonia nearby. Taiwanese techwear brands like oqLiq and WISDOM — functional waterproof clothing designed for Taiwan's humidity, with a cyberpunk aesthetic — are found in Xinyi and Zhongshan districts if you want local design labels.
Allow 1–2 hours. Prices are competitive. The Deng Shan You discount rack is worth digging through.

Late Night · Optional
After the Red House, walk 5 min to Huaxi Street Night Market for a late bite at Yuan Fang Gua Bao 源芳刈包 — the peanut powder pork bun you wanted.
Yuan Fang Gua Bao 源芳刈包 · Huaxi Street Night Market
📍 No. 17-4, Huaxi St, Wanhua District (steps from Longshan Temple) 🕐 Open from ~4pm · food stalls close by 10pm 💰 ~NT$60 per guabao
Huaxi Street (historically "Snake Alley") is Taipei's oldest covered night market — a pedestrian lane with a distinctly old-school, local character right next to Longshan Temple. The one food item that makes the detour completely worthwhile: Yuan Fang Gua Bao, a Michelin Bib Gourmand stall that has been making the same guabao (刈包, "Taiwanese hamburger") for over 60 years. Each one is a pillowy steamed bun folded around a thick slab of fatty pork belly braised in soy sauce, topped with tangy pickled mustard greens, fresh cilantro, and a generous shower of ground peanut powder — the peanut powder melts into the fat of the pork and creates something genuinely addictive. They also do four types of traditional herbal soup.

While you're there: Wang's Broth 小王煮瓜 next door is a Michelin Bib Gourmand braised pork rice stall that's been here since 1975 — if you have room, a small bowl of the braised pork and mushroom rice alongside the guabao is a full Wanhua late-night meal for about NT$150 total.
Get there before 9:30pm — stalls begin closing down. Huaxi is small, covered, and far less crowded than Raohe or Shilin, which makes the whole experience feel more genuine and local.

Temple · Trail Start
Songshan Fengtian Temple 松山奉天宮 — MRT to Houshanpi station or Songshan station, short taxi or 15-min walk to the temple at the base of Tiger Mountain.
Songshan Fengtian Temple 松山奉天宮
📍 Base of Tiger Mountain, Songshan District (also trailhead for Four Beasts) 🕐 Open daily 💰 Free
One of the most architecturally extraordinary temples in Taipei — dedicated to the Jade Emperor, the supreme deity in the Chinese pantheon. The scale is immediately arresting: the principal columns in the main hall soar over 20 meters high. The exterior features a grandiose ceremonial archway, a famous nine-dragon wall (九龍壁) relief carving, and stone columns and wooden beams covered in carvings of dragons, phoenixes, and scenes from classical folktales. A golden eight-trigrams (八卦) pattern decorates the roof. A censer cast from nickel and copper (the material of coins) sits at the entrance. This temple is backed directly against Tiger Mountain — the Sishoushan Four Beasts hiking trail system begins from the temple grounds, making it one of the most dramatically situated urban temples in Taiwan.
Spend 20–30 minutes here before heading up the trail. The morning incense smoke and the vertical scale of the columns in low light is particularly striking.
8:45am
Hike · Full Loop
Four Beasts Mountain loop → Elephant Mountain viewpoint — trails begin from the temple grounds.
Four Beasts Mountain → Elephant Mountain 四獸山步道 → 象山步道
📍 Songshan / Xinyi District ⏱ 2–3 hrs full loop 💰 Free 🥾 Beginner to intermediate · stone steps and ridge trail
Hike the Four Beasts loop from the Fangtian Temple end (Tiger Mountain → Leopard → Lion) across the ridge, then descend to Elephant Mountain (象山) for the classic Taipei 101 skyline viewpoint — the one in every photo. Doing the route this direction means you approach the famous viewpoint from above rather than fighting uphill with the crowds, and you experience the full ridge traverse with city views in all directions before the money shot at the end. From the Six Boulders Lookout (Elephant Mountain), look straight down the ridge at Taipei 101 and the Xinyi skyline. Descend to Xiangshan MRT station (10 min down).
Start from the Fangtian Temple end so the Elephant Mountain viewpoint is your finish line rather than your turnaround. Much more satisfying arc, and you'll hit the viewpoint before the midday crowds build.
11:30am
Neighborhood · Lunch
MRT from Xiangshan to Dongmen → Yongkang Street (永康街, Exit 5, 2-min walk). Taipei's most livable neighborhood for a slow afternoon.
Yongkang Street 永康街
📍 Da'an District 🚇 Dongmen MRT (Red + Green Lines) Exit 5
A tree-lined street of independent cafés in old Japanese-era houses, design boutiques, bookshops, and back-alley murals — Taipei's most livable and artsy neighborhood for a long afternoon browse. Food highlights: Din Tai Fung 鼎泰豐 (No. 194, Sec. 2, Xinyi Rd) — the original, legendary xiao long bao location, worth it for soup dumplings alone; arrive before 11:30am or after 2pm for shortest wait. Yongkang Beef Noodle 永康牛肉麵 (No. 17, Lane 31, Jinhua St) — the local candidate for Taipei's best beef noodle soup, rich spiced broth, meltingly tender beef. Papaya milk — available at several fruit stalls on Yongkang itself; look for fresh-squeezed signs (木瓜牛奶). Get it cold, thick, and unsweetened if possible.
Papaya milk is one of those things that tastes exactly as good as it sounds when made fresh — ripe papaya blended with cold milk, thick and fragrant. Any stall with fresh fruit visible is the right choice.
2:30pm
Park
Da'an Forest Park 大安森林公園 — 5-min walk from the south end of Yongkang Street.
Da'an Forest Park 大安森林公園
📍 Da'an District 🕐 Open 24 hrs 💰 Free 🚇 Da'an Forest Park MRT (Red Line)
Taipei's Central Park — 26 hectares of urban forest in the heart of the city, with a large lake, performance bandstand, jogging paths, giant banyan trees, and a resident population of wild grey herons that nest in the treetops. In November the park has a particular golden quality in the afternoon light. A complete contrast to the commercial energy of Yongkang just steps away. Walk the lake circuit, watch the herons, and let the post-hike afternoon settle. About 30–45 minutes at a relaxed pace.
The bandstand hosts occasional free weekend performances — check what's on. The herons are enormous and completely unbothered by people.
5:30pm
Temple + Sunset View · Optional
Bishanyan Kaizhang Shengwang Temple — MRT Brown Line to Neihu, then bus S2 (8 min) to the Bishanyan stop.
Bishanyan Kaizhang Shengwang Temple 碧山巖開漳聖王廟
📍 Mt. Bishan, Neihu District 🕐 Open daily 5am–9:30pm 💰 Free 🚇 MRT Brown Line to Neihu → Bus S2 to Bishanyan stop (~8 min)
One of Taipei's most visually striking temples and one of its least-known great viewpoints. Built in 1751 on the summit of Mt. Bishan in Neihu, the temple is dedicated to Chen Yuan-Kuang — a Tang Dynasty general revered as a patron saint by Fujianese settlers — and its architecture is extraordinary: vivid painted murals across every beam, hand-carved dragons coiling along the roof ridges, and intricate tilework covering the entire complex in an explosion of color. But the real revelation is the panoramic viewing platform: from the balcony in front of the main hall you look out over the entire Taipei Basin — Taipei 101 directly ahead, the Grand Hotel to the northwest, Songshan Airport to the northeast, the Keelung River winding below, and on clear days the mountains all the way to the coast. It's a completely different angle from Elephant Mountain, and arguably more dramatic because you're surrounded by forest rather than city.

The best time to visit is the hour before sunset: the sky transitions from blue to gold to pink above downtown Taipei, and the city lights begin coming on as you watch. After dark the city lights spread out like a circuit board — stunning and entirely free.
Arrive at least 1.5 hrs before sunset on weekends for a good railing spot. Weekday afternoons are much quieter. There's a small café inside the temple (donation-based coffee) with the same view.
The S2 shuttle bus from Neihu MRT is the easiest approach — winds up the mountain road and drops you at the temple entrance. The bus driver handles the hairpin curves with cheerful confidence.
7:30pm
Night Market
Raohe Night Market (饒河夜市) — MRT to Songshan station, 1 stop. (If skipping Bishanyan, start Raohe earlier at 6pm.)
Songshan Ciyou Temple 松山慈祐宮 — Lanterns First
📍 At the Raohe Street entrance to the night market 🕐 Open daily, lanterns lit from dusk 💰 Free
Arrive at the Raohe end (Ciyou Temple entrance) at dusk before diving into the market — the temple's triple-eave roof with its elaborate cut-and-paste ceramic figurines (dragons, phoenixes, historical scenes in vivid glazed ceramic) glows in the lantern light at this hour in a way that photographs can't fully capture. Built in 1753, it houses one of Taipei's oldest stone censers (over 200 years old) and a pair of door carvings from the early 18th century. Spend 15 minutes here before the market swallows you.
Raohe Night Market 饒河街夜市 — What to Eat
📍 Songshan District 🕐 Open daily ~5pm–midnight 🚇 Songshan MRT (Green Line)
A 600-meter covered market between two temple gates — one of Taipei's best for actual eating. Your specific targets: Black pepper buns (胡椒餅) at the oven stall near the Ciyou Temple entrance end — thick pork buns baked in a wood-fired clay oven, charred outside, juicy within, heavy with black pepper. The queue is always long and always moving fast. Get one immediately.

King oyster mushroom skewers (杏鮑菇) — look for the grilled mushroom stalls partway down the market. King oyster mushrooms sliced thick, grilled over charcoal, brushed with a sweet-savory sauce, sometimes with cheese melted on top. They're meaty, umami-rich, and one of the best vegetarian-friendly market foods in Taiwan. Medicinal herb soup, braised pig's feet, and stinky tofu (much stronger than you expect) round out the classic hits.
Enter from the Ciyou Temple end — the black pepper bun stall is right there. Work your way south toward the other temple gate. The mushroom stalls are in the middle stretch of the market.

8:00am
Hike
Yangmingshan National Park — Bus from Jiantan MRT (Red Line) direct to the park. Multiple bus routes available from the Jiantan bus stop.
Yangmingshan National Park 陽明山國家公園
📍 North Taipei ⏱ 2–4 hrs depending on trail 💰 Free 🥾 Beginner to intermediate
A volcanic national park sitting directly above Taipei's northern suburbs — sulfur fumaroles, hot springs, and extraordinary highland scenery. In November, the silver grass (芒草) is in full bloom across the hillsides, turning the landscape into something out of a Japanese film.

Recommended for your fitness level: The Erziping Trail (二子坪步道) — flat, accessible, 2-km loop through misty highland meadows with volcanic landscape and the famous sulfur fumaroles at Xiaoyoukeng nearby. Entirely different in character from Elephant Mountain — here you're above the clouds rather than looking up at a skyline.

For more challenge: Qixingshan (七星山, Seven Star Mountain) at 1,120m — the highest peak in Taipei, moderate 3-hr round trip with steep stone steps and 360° views. Stunning but commit to an early start.
Free foot-soaking hot spring pool at Lengshuikeng visitor center on the descent — natural geothermal water, wooden benches, local elderly crowd, completely free. One of the most pleasant 20 minutes in Taipei.
1:00pm
Vintage Street
Return to Jiantan MRT → MRT to Zhongshan → Chifeng Street (exit 4, 2-min walk).
Chifeng Street 赤峰街 — Taipei's Vintage / Creative Lane
📍 Zhongshan District, Datong District border 🕐 Most shops open 1–9pm (many open afternoons only) 🚇 MRT Zhongshan Station (Red Line) Exit 4 or Shuanglian Exit 1
One of Taipei's best streets for wandering without a plan. Chifeng was historically known as "Blacksmith Street" — a mid-century district of iron workshops and auto-parts dealers. Over the past decade it has transformed completely: old workshops became minimalist cafés, hardware stores turned into indie boutiques, vintage shops share space with art studios. The physical texture of the street survived the transformation — the lanes are narrow, the buildings are low, the motorbikes are parked between tiny storefronts — giving it a genuinely neighborhood feel that the more polished parts of Zhongshan lack.

What you'll find: PAR Store (Lane 3 — seriously curated vintage clothing in a hidden alley building), EWF Vintage (Lane 49 — international vintage including Mexican dresses, Japanese haori, Austrian dirndls, and Carhartt), Travis Vintage (Lane 41 — reworked US military jackets from the 1930s–90s, Hawaiian shirts, excellent graphic tees), Banana Cats (Lane 49 — blazers, leather goods, original jewelry, plus cats), and Coffee Dumbo (Lane 49 — retro café with cinnamon rolls, great for a break mid-browse). Vinyl records, vintage cameras, Japanese 80s–90s pieces, and ceramic art objects throughout.
Many shops don't open until 1–2pm. This is ideal timing — arrive after lunch when the street is at its most animated. Allow 1–1.5 hrs to explore the lanes properly; each side alley has more to discover.
2:30pm
Historic Market Street
Walk 10 minutes north to Dihua Street (Chifeng and Dihua are in the same Zhongshan/Datong zone — very walkable between them).
Dihua Street 迪化街 + Dadaocheng District
📍 Datong District — 10-min walk north of Chifeng St 🕐 Most shops open 10am–6pm
Taipei's oldest commercial street, in a neighborhood called Dadaocheng — once Taiwan's most important trade port. The Baroque shophouse facades now house a wonderful mix: traditional dried goods and tea merchants alongside indie design pop-ups, concept cafés, and fabric stores. It's one of the few places in Taipei where you can feel the city's actual age. Browse at will — buy tea, dried mango, fabric.
The small Xiahai City God Temple (霞海城隍廟) at the north end is one of the most popular matchmaking temples in Taiwan — there's usually a long prayer line. Worth a quick look.
4:30pm
Hidden Gem
Dadaocheng Wharf / Pier 5 — walk 10 minutes west from Dihua Street down to the Tamsui River.
Dadaocheng Wharf 大稻埕碼頭 (Pier 5)
📍 West end of Dihua St., Tamsui River 🕐 Late afternoon into evening — food stalls open ~4:30pm 💰 Free
The single most underrated sunset spot in Taipei that tourists consistently miss. From late afternoon the riverside pier transforms: shipping containers converted to bars, craft beer stalls (look for Sunmai Brewery — their sour ales are excellent), Taiwanese street food, live music, and locals gathering at the river's edge as the sky turns. The Tamsui River reflects the pink and orange light perfectly. It has the feeling of a neighborhood block party that happens to have extraordinary views. Completely unpretentious, entirely local, and genuinely beautiful.
Budget 1.5–2 hours here. This is the kind of place you'll be thinking about on the plane home. Bring a light jacket as it cools by the river after dark.
7:30pm
Night Market
Ningxia Night Market (寧夏夜市) — 15-minute walk south from the wharf. Taipei's most beloved local night market for traditional Taiwanese snacks: braised pork rice (the definitive version), oyster omelet, aiyu jelly with lemon, red bean cakes. Compact, never overwhelming, always delicious. Ends around midnight.

Teapot Mountain 茶壺山 · Jinguashi Gold Ecological Park
📍 Jinguashi, New Taipei City ⏱ 2–3 hrs total 💰 Free trail (park entrance free if hiking only) 🥾 Intermediate — involves rock scrambling
One of the most satisfying hikes near Taipei: the trail winds up through a former gold mining area to a distinctive cluster of rocks shaped exactly like a teapot, with a cave-like passage through the "spout" that you physically climb through. The views from the top look down on the coastline, the ruined mining infrastructure, and the extraordinary Yin-Yang Sea directly below.

The Yin-Yang Sea (陰陽海) is a natural phenomenon caused by iron sulfide seeping from the old mine drainage — the ocean turns a striking blue-gold at the point where the mineral water meets the sea. You'll see it clearly from the Teapot Mountain ridgeline and from the road below. It looks almost CGI. There's a viewpoint platform on the main road specifically for photographs.

The Jinguashi Gold Ecological Park itself is fascinating — the remains of a former Japanese-era gold mine with preserved infrastructure, a museum, and a free touchable gold bar (the largest single piece you can legally touch in the world at 220kg).
The cave passage through the "teapot" requires some scrambling and ducking — not technical, but not for anyone with claustrophobia. The longer Banpingshan extension (adds 1 hr, more scrambling, ropes on cliff sections) is spectacular if you want more challenge.

Erliao Sunrise Pavilion 二寮觀景台
📍 Zuozhen District, southeastern Tainan — ~64km from city center 🕐 Open 24 hours 💰 Free 🚗 Car or taxi only — no public transport
The lowest-elevation cloud-sea sunrise spot in Taiwan — known as "the sunrise closest to the city." The pavilion is a large wooden observation deck above a chalk-terrain bamboo forest valley in the Siraya National Scenic Area. Before dawn the sky is dark and starry over the Central Mountain Range; as first light comes, the sun rises from behind the mountains and golden rays scatter across the bamboo and mist below in patterns that have been compared to Chinese ink-wash painting. On the best mornings a sea of clouds settles in the valley and the sun burns through it in shafts of light. Even on cloudy days, the fog lifting from the bamboo groves at dawn is described by visitors as mesmerizing.

Among Taiwan's three famous low-altitude cloud sunrise spots — Getou in the north, Wucheng in the center, and Erliao in the south — Erliao is the most accessible from a southern base. The platform can accommodate nearly 2,000 people, so there's room even on busy days — though serious photographers arrive from 2am for tripod position. The drive through the predawn hills is part of the experience.
November is outside the peak season (June–September) but still produces good sunrises on clear days — check weather the night before. If it rained the previous day, the morning fog is often exceptional. Bring a jacket — it's chilly at altitude before dawn.

Sicao Green Tunnel 四草綠色隧道 — "Taiwan's Little Amazon"
📍 Annan District, Tainan (Sicao Dazhong Temple) 🕐 Open daily 8am–4pm 💰 NT$200 per person (Mangrove Tunnel route) ⏱ 30–40 min boat ride + 30 min visit time
One of the most remarkable natural experiences in southern Taiwan, and a genuine gap in most Tainan itineraries. A bamboo raft glides through a waterway where four species of 50-year-old native mangroves have grown together overhead, their branches interlocking to form a dense, cathedral-like green canopy. The light filters through in patterns; egrets and black-crowned night herons fly alongside at eye level; fiddler crabs move through the roots. At the tunnel's deepest point, the still water creates a perfect mirror reflection of the canopy — an optical illusion called the "Angel's Kiss" or "Green Eye" where the boundary between tree and reflection disappears entirely.

The experience is genuinely transportive. Guides narrate (mostly Mandarin, but the biology signs are in English and the visual experience speaks for itself). The adjacent free Sperm Whale Museum — entered with your ticket stub — houses complete specimens of a stranded mother and calf sperm whale that beached nearby. Unexpected and genuinely moving.
Go at 8am opening: cooler, fewer people, and the morning light through the canopy is extraordinary. Bring bug spray. The guides are enthusiastic and knowledgeable — the boat commentary is worth a translation attempt through your phone if you're curious.
This is part of Taijiang National Park (台江國家公園) — one of Taiwan's least-visited national parks despite being extraordinary. The wetlands protect over 240 species of fish, 200+ shellfish species, and are critical habitat for the endangered black-faced spoonbill which winters here Nov–March. You may see them from the boat.

Shoushan Nature Park 壽山自然公園 ("Monkey Mountain")
📍 West Kaohsiung — directly above the harbor ⏱ 2–3 hrs depending on route 💰 Free 🥾 Moderate — steep in sections, mostly well-paved
Kaohsiung's best nature experience and the hike that most visitors miss entirely. Shoushan is a limestone ridge rising directly out of the city on its western edge, home to a large population of wild Formosan rock macaques — Taiwan's only native primate — that roam freely along every trail. You will walk alongside troops of them: mothers with infants, juveniles playing in the trees, elders sitting on boulders surveying the trail with complete indifference to your presence. This kind of proximity to wild primates in an urban setting is genuinely unusual anywhere in the world.

From the ridgeline, views open over the entire Kaohsiung harbor, the Love River delta, Lotus Pond to the north, and on clear days the Taiwan Strait to the west. The contrast of wild forest and working port city below is completely distinctive to Kaohsiung.

Recommended route: Enter from the Shoushan Zoo (zoo entrance, trail continues past the zoo gate into the mountain). The main Zhongzheng Hiking Trail reaches the ridgeline in about 40 minutes. Turn north along the ridge for the harbor views. Return by the same path or loop via Chaishan Road on the east slope.
⚠️ Do NOT carry visible food. The macaques are experienced thieves and move fast. Keep snacks deep in your bag and zipped. They will not harm you if you don't have food — they're completely habituated to hikers. Feed them at your own risk (it's also bad for them ecologically).
Go early (7:30–8am) before the heat builds. November is comfortable but the limestone can be slippery after the rare rain. The morning light through the forest is beautiful.

Yancheng First Public Retail Market 鹽埕第一公有零售市場
📍 No. 141-7, Lainan St, Yancheng District 🕐 Morning–5pm (most stalls) · afternoon food stalls from ~1pm 💰 Free entry 🚇 Orange Line to Yanchengpu, or walk from Pier 2
Established in 1949 and once the most prosperous traditional market in Kaohsiung, the Yancheng First Public Market underwent a major redesign in 2022 — restoring its original wooden gable roof structure, adding skylights and ventilation, and opening the space to new younger businesses while keeping the traditional stalls that have been here for decades. The result is a rare market that genuinely works on two levels simultaneously: traditional wet market vendors selling fresh produce and noodles in the morning alongside new concept stalls with craft beer, whiskey shots, art goods, and indie food in the afternoon.

The alleyways surrounding the market are bustling with retro bars, thrift and second-hand shops, and top-rated food stalls popular with local youth. Dagouding Old Street (大溝頂) runs nearby with a few preserved Japanese-era buildings and a casual arts cluster. The whole Yancheng neighborhood is compact, flat, comfortably walkable, and has become Kaohsiung's most powerful tourist magnet after years of creative renaissance.
Go in the early afternoon (1–3pm) when the market transitions from traditional stalls to the more creative afternoon vendors — the best window for art, craft, and food. Most everything closes by 5pm so don't leave it too late.

Temple Complex

Lotus Lake 蓮池潭 — Dragon & Tiger Pagodas
📍 Zuoying District, north Kaohsiung 🕐 Pagodas open daily 8am–5pm 💰 Free ⚠️ Dragon & Tiger Pagodas fully reopened 2025 after renovation
A man-made lake ringed by brightly colored temples, pavilions, and religious architecture of maximum visual intensity. The anchor: the Dragon and Tiger Pagodas — two seven-story towers rising from the water, one a dragon (enter through its open mouth), one a tiger (exit through its mouth), with elaborate hand-painted murals inside depicting heaven and hell in vivid detail. Kaohsiung folk religion at its most theatrical.

The Spring and Autumn Pavilions nearby (connected to the lake by a zigzag bridge) house a famous Xuantian Supreme Emperor statue and sit over the water on painted causeway bridges. The whole complex is maximalist religious art at a scale you won't find anywhere else — it rewards wandering and looking closely at the painted detail rather than rushing through for photos.
Allow 1.5 hours minimum for the full circuit. Enter the Dragon's mouth, walk the murals inside, exit the Tiger — this is the traditional sequence said to transform bad luck into good. Even if you're skeptical, the murals alone are worth it.

Ciaotou Refinery MRT station → Qiaotou Sugar Factory.
Qiaotou Sugar Factory 橋頭糖廠
📍 Qiaotou District — Ciaotou Refinery MRT Station 🕐 Open daily 9am–5pm 💰 Free
Taiwan's oldest sugar refinery (1901, built by the Japanese colonial administration) operated continuously until 1999 when the last crop was processed and the factory went silent. Now an industrial heritage park, entirely free to explore: original narrow-gauge railway carriages on rusting tracks through the grounds, factory buildings in varying states of photogenic decay, koi ponds, old colonial staff housing converted to small shops, and public art installations scattered throughout. Guerrilla murals appear in every corner — the peeling paint, aged metal, and overgrown grounds make it one of the most atmosphere-rich spaces in Kaohsiung.

Mikan the station cat — the resident station cat, adopted by MRT staff and now a minor celebrity with a cat-themed bakery next to the MRT exit and a proper station employee badge. Worth five minutes of your day purely for the delight of it.
Best in morning light when the long shadows fall across the old railway tracks. The ice cream stall sells sugarcane-flavored soft serve made from the refinery's last batch of sugar — iconic.

Guandu Temple 關渡宮
📍 Guandu, Beitou District 🕐 Open daily 6am–9pm 💰 Free 🚇 Guandu MRT (Red Line, end of the Xinbeitou branch)
One of the most dramatically situated temples in all of Taiwan — a 1661 Matsu temple built directly into the face of a cliff above the Tamsui River, where the river bends toward the sea. The main temple hall fronts the water; behind it, a winding cave tunnel carved into the rock interior passes through a series of small shrines, altars, and stone-carved reliefs in near-darkness before emerging on the other side of the cliff into a manicured garden with pruned trees and harbor views below. The transition from sunlit incense-filled temple courtyard into a rock-carved underground passage into a secret cliff garden is one of the most surprising architectural sequences in Taipei.

Guandu Wharf is a 10-minute walk downhill from the temple — a quiet riverside pier with morning food stalls, bamboo fishing rafts, and views over the Tamsui River wetlands toward the sea. Very local, very unhurried.
Walk through the cave passage even if you're not religious — the transition from the incense-smoky outer hall through the carved rock interior is extraordinary. Allow 45 minutes for the full temple complex and 30 minutes at the wharf.
